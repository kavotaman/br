About

Otávio Manzano Kavakama began his musical studies at the age of seven, with piano and recorder teacher Eliane, at the House of Culture Jandyra Massoni, in Atibaia, Brazil. At the house of José Ricardo, still in Atibaia, Brazil he plucked his way through early adolescence, accompanied by the esteemed Gabriel, today enjoying the cool breeze of the subtropical climate.

From Love Me Do to The Number of the Beast, Otávio developed jugular muscles and started to fall asleep to the sound of dying high frequencies. At a local music school, he goes from student to professor after a few years — path that included being an occasional dweller and moving-in-and-out helper.

While witnessing a concert of the European tradition kind, it came to his attention the genesis of a newly formed orchestra class. Wishing for some friction in his life, Manzano soon develops an interest for the supposedly high-pitch descendant of the lira da braccio. After asking about the possibility of applying for such sonic artifact, he was asked back if he would not consider applying for an alternative artifact. The artifact in question was the little-big-progeny of the viola da braccio. Indifferent regarding the different sizes, the aforementioned subject of this bio, named after the first Roman emperor, confirmed his application.

After a 5-year period in which Kavakama played, among many things, major scales and arpeggios, he moved to the birthplace of Blues and Rock, where he learned some Blues and Rock, but mostly some old white European music writers; all under the guidance of Alexander Russakovsky. For the desperate ears of his neighbors, family members, and dogs, Manzano suddenly decided to focus his attention in what really matters: music of the present. This unforeseen change in life path occurred after a supposedly Western Art [sic] Contemporary Music mosquito bit both his arms. These arms now refuse to play Bach.

All the best,

Otávio
